# 1. The Premise, Stated in Its Crudest Form
A grandmother in Minnesota, watching her husband refuse to consult the map for the third time on a four-hour drive that should have taken three, says: “Men are stubborn. Women think.” She delivers this without rancor, in the resigned tone of someone making an observation about the weather. Her husband, hearing her, does not consult the map. He is busy demonstrating her point.

The line, in this version, is sharper than the more familiar “Men are stubborn, women are smart.” “Smart” is about intelligence, a property the cognitive scientists have measured carefully and found to be distributed equally between the sexes. “Think” is about something different: about deliberation, reconsideration, willingness to pause an action in progress and ask whether the chosen course is still the right one. It is the difference, in the dual-process framework of contemporary cognitive psychology, between System 1 (fast, intuitive, automatic) and System 2 (slow, deliberative, reflective). The grandmother is not claiming her husband cannot think. She is claiming he is not, at this moment, thinking. He is acting on the System 1 conviction that he knows where he is going.

Restated this way, the stereotype is closer to something the empirical literature can actually support. The behavioral-science work of the past forty years has documented several robust average differences in deliberation, risk assessment, help-seeking, and the willingness to interrupt a chosen course in light of new evidence — differences which, in everyday situations like the one in the car, produce exactly the pattern the grandmother is describing. This paper takes her observation seriously, asks what the research actually shows, and asks what is left of the stereotype when the data are in. The short answer is that the grandmother is right on average, wrong in the particular, and entirely justified in the satisfaction she takes when, twenty minutes later, her husband finally pulls into a gas station.

# 2. Where the Stereotype Earns Its Keep
Several decades of careful study, summarized in Table 1, have documented average sex differences in directions consistent with the stereotype. None of these is a clean two-population separation; they are distributional differences with substantial overlap. But the direction of the average difference, in the domains the stereotype targets, is consistent.

*Table 1. Sex differences relevant to the stubborn-versus-thinking stereotype, with rough effect sizes from meta-analyses.*

| **Domain**                                      | **Direction of average difference** | **Effect size**                    |
|-----------------------|--------------------------|-----------------------|
| Stopping to reconsider a chosen course          | Women > men                      | Moderate (d ≈ 0.30–0.50)           |
| Loss aversion in financial decisions            | Women > men                      | Small to moderate (d ≈ 0.20–0.40)  |
| Help-seeking (medical, navigational, technical) | Women > men                      | Moderate (d ≈ 0.30–0.50)           |
| Physical risk-taking (driving, sports)          | Men > women                      | Moderate to large (d ≈ 0.40–0.80)  |
| Mortality from preventable causes < age 75   | Men > women                      | Large (US: ≈ 1.7 : 1)              |
| Trading frequency / portfolio turnover          | Men > women                      | Moderate (≈45% more trades by men) |
| Reading nonverbal & emotional cues              | Women > men                      | Small to moderate (d ≈ 0.20–0.40)  |
| Persistence on failing strategies (lab tasks)   | Men > women                      | Small (d ≈ 0.10–0.20)              |

The most direct evidence for the “thinking” claim comes from work on deliberation in financial decision-making. Barber and Odean’s well-known 2001 study of more than 35,000 retail investors found that men trade their stock portfolios 45 percent more often than women, and that this overactive trading reduces net returns. The men are not failing because they cannot think; they are failing because they do not pause to think. They act on the System 1 conviction that they know which stock is about to move, they execute the trade, and they move on to the next conviction. The women, on average, take longer between trades, hold positions longer, second-guess their initial impressions more often, and — by the simple expedient of doing less — do better. Subsequent work in many markets has confirmed the basic pattern.

A second strand of evidence concerns loss aversion. A 2023 study from the University of Bath (Dawson, 2023) found that more than half (53%) of the male-female risk-taking gap in financial decisions is attributable to greater loss aversion in women: women feel potential losses more acutely than men do, and the prospect of a loss triggers in them the deliberative System 2 evaluation that men, on average, skip. Men do not lack the capacity for this evaluation; they fail to trigger it under conditions where women routinely do. The asymmetry is in the threshold for stopping to think, not in the ability to think well once stopped.

A third strand of evidence is the help-seeking gap. Men, particularly men socialized into traditional masculine norms, are less likely than women to seek medical care, less likely to consult a mental-health professional, less likely to read instruction manuals, less likely to ask a younger relative for help with a new technology — and less likely to ask for directions. Addis and Mahalik’s 2003 review of this literature found the pattern in essentially every population studied; the effect is moderate in size (d ≈ 0.30 to 0.50) and remarkably consistent across cultures, age groups, and specific help-seeking domains. Asking for help is itself a form of thinking — it requires recognizing one has reached the limit of one’s own competence, pausing the chosen course, and admitting the need for external input. Men, on average, are slower to do this than women are.

A fourth strand, less often discussed but well-documented, is sex differences in empathic accuracy. Women, on average, score modestly higher on tasks that require reading another person’s emotional state from facial expression, tone of voice, or contextual cues. The advantage is small (d ≈ 0.2 to 0.4) and disappears under some experimental conditions — notably when emotional content is task-irrelevant, suggesting the advantage reflects attention and motivation rather than raw perceptual ability. But it bears on the grandmother’s claim. The woman in the front passenger seat who can tell, from her husband’s tone, that he is lost and unwilling to admit it, is performing exactly the kind of System 2 reading-of-the-situation that the data say women do somewhat more reliably than men.

These four strands — deliberation in financial choice, loss aversion, help-seeking, and empathic accuracy — are not exotic findings from a single fragile study. They are robust patterns documented across decades and across cultures, with effect sizes that meta-analyses have stabilized in the small-to-moderate range. They share a common shape: in domains where stopping to think before acting is the more profitable strategy, women, on average, do it more often. The stereotype has its empirical foundation in this small-to-moderate but real pattern.

# 3. Where the Stereotype Falls Apart
For each domain where the stereotype gains some empirical ground, there is at least one domain where it fails badly. Table 2 enumerates the principal failures.

*Table 2. Common claims of the stubborn-versus-thinking stereotype that do not survive contact with the data.*

| **Claim or context**                  | **What the evidence actually shows**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
|------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| "Women think and men don't"           | False in the strong form. Both sexes are equally capable of deliberation; the difference is in the threshold at which deliberation gets triggered, and in the situations where each sex tends to default to fast intuitive judgment instead. (Kahneman 2011; Stanovich 2011.) |
| "Women are more intelligent"          | No meaningful sex difference in general intelligence (g) on standardized testing. Some small subtest differences both directions; overall means essentially equal. (Halpern et al. 2007; Hyde 2014.)                                                                          |
| "Men are reckless, women are careful" | Holds in financial and physical-risk domains in Western samples. In small-scale traditional and matrilineal societies the gap shrinks or reverses. The pattern is biology amplified by culture, not biology alone. (Pondorfer et al. 2016; Booth & Nolen 2012.)               |
| "Men won't ask for directions"        | A real but small phenomenon. Men report less willingness to seek help across many domains, but the effect overlaps massively between the sexes. The stereotype is exaggerated relative to the data. (Addis & Mahalik 2003.)                                                   |
| "Men are overconfident"               | Robust finding in financial and self-assessment tasks. Men overestimate their abilities and the precision of their estimates more than women do, and act on those overestimates rather than pausing to test them. (Barber & Odean 2001.)                                      |
| "All of this is biological"           | Effect sizes vary substantially by culture, by age cohort, and by historical period. Single-sex schooling reduces some risk-aversion gaps. The pattern is biology plus cultural amplification, not biology alone. (Booth & Nolen 2012.)                                       |

The most important failure is taking the “women think, men don’t” claim in its strong form. The data say nothing of the kind. Both sexes have equivalent System 2 capacity; the difference is in the threshold at which System 2 is recruited. Men, on average, are slower to interrupt an action in progress to evaluate whether it is still the right action. They are not less able to evaluate; they are less inclined to pause. In domains where System 2 is engaged by default — a man working through a difficult mathematical problem, a man rebuilding a transmission, a man writing a careful paper — there is no observed deliberation deficit. The deficit is specifically in the willingness to stop a chosen action and reconsider it. Stubbornness is the precise word.

A second failure is the assumption that the patterns of Section 2 are simply biological. Cross-cultural work shows that the risk-aversion gap shrinks substantially in matrilineal societies and small-scale traditional cultures. Single-sex schooling reduces it in adolescents. The gap appears to be biology amplified by culture rather than biology alone, and the cultural amplification is not constant across history or geography. A 21st-century American urban professional woman is much closer in risk-taking behavior to her male colleagues than her own grandmother was to her grandfather. Whatever sex differences exist in the threshold for deliberation, they are not destiny.

A third failure, which deserves emphasis, is the ecological fallacy — the error of inferring an individual’s behavior from group-average data. The grandmother is observing average tendencies; she generalizes to her particular husband, who happens to fit the average. But fully 30 to 40 percent of men, on most of the measures in Table 1, fall on the female side of the median female value. Conversely, 30 to 40 percent of women fall on the male side of the median male value. The distributions overlap so heavily that knowing a person’s sex tells you very little about their actual decisions in any specific situation. The stereotype is a real average pattern misapplied to specific cases where it often does not hold.

# 4. Stubbornness as a Strategy, Not a Defect
A subtler treatment of the stereotype recognizes that the male tendency to persist on a chosen course is sometimes the source of significant accomplishment as well as significant disaster. The same disposition that produces a man who refuses to admit he is lost also produces, on a different day, the man who refuses to give up on a difficult problem at work, who keeps trying after the first three approaches have failed, who finishes the project nobody else thought could be finished. Persistence past the point where deliberation would suggest retreat is a mixed strategy; sometimes it pays off and sometimes it does not.

The retail-investment data discussed earlier are the clearest empirical version of the stereotype. Male portfolios underperform female portfolios on average because the men trade too often, on too little reflection. But the same overconfidence-and-action pattern that produces underperformance in retail investing produces, in a different domain, the long-shot entrepreneurial successes that drive much of the economy. The man who is wrongly convinced he can build a successful company despite four previous failures is, in aggregate, a poor bet. But the small fraction of those men who are right — perhaps one in twenty — produces a disproportionate share of the new firms, new technologies, and new wealth. A culture that selects strictly for cautious deliberation may, in this domain, leave value on the table.

Most dispositions are virtues in some contexts and vices in others. The willingness to pause and reconsider is straightforwardly valuable on a Sunday drive in unfamiliar country; it is much less valuable in the workshop where the man rebuilt the Chevrolet, where finishing the difficult thing required precisely the refusal to give up on the chosen course. The grandmother is correct about her husband on the Sunday drive and entirely wrong about him on the question of whether he has been a worthwhile presence in her life. His disposition is the same in both situations; the situations call for different things from it.

# 5. What the Grandmother Actually Knows
The stereotype, as articulated by the grandmother, is a compressed version of a more nuanced observation that the literature largely confirms. Restated less crudely, it would read approximately as follows.

Men, on average, are somewhat slower than women to interrupt an action in progress and ask whether the chosen course is still the right one; somewhat more confident in their own judgment than the situation warrants; somewhat more willing to take risks that produce both large successes and large failures; and somewhat less skilled at, or less motivated to deploy, the kind of social perception that reads others’ emotional states accurately. Women, on average, are somewhat quicker to pause a chosen course and reconsider it; somewhat more sensitive to potential losses and therefore more cautious; somewhat better at, or more motivated to deploy, social perception; and consequently somewhat better at the kind of practical day-to-day judgment that involves correctly identifying when one is in trouble and acting on that knowledge.

None of these differences is large. None of them maps onto a difference in intelligence or in the capacity for thought. They are differences in the threshold for activating deliberation, in the weight assigned to potential losses, and in the motivation to read social context. But the pattern is real enough to give the grandmother’s observation an empirical foundation, and old enough to have generated the folk shorthand by which she expresses it. She is making, in a single sentence, a compact summary of about eighty years of behavioral-science findings. She has not read the studies. She has watched her husband try to find her sister’s house without consulting the map. The data and the observation converge on the same conclusion, by very different routes.

The husband, for his part, may take some comfort from the literature. His stubbornness is not unique to him, is not a personal failing, is partly biological, and has produced, over a long life of similar episodes, both the embarrassments his wife remembers and the achievements she also remembers but is less likely to mention while they are lost on a county road. His wife’s superior judgment in the present moment is not a permanent ranking of their respective cognitive abilities; it is a specific instance of a small average advantage applied to a domain where her disposition fits the problem better than his does. On another day, in another situation, the rankings will reverse. The grandmother knows this too, although she will not say it while they are still lost.

# 6. Conclusion
Folk stereotypes about sex differences are usually wrong in the strong form and right in the weak form. “Men are stubborn, women think”, in the strong form, claims that men lack the capacity for deliberation. They do not. In the weak form, it points at a set of real average differences — in the threshold for triggering deliberation, in the depth of loss aversion, in the willingness to seek help, in the routine deployment of social perception — that show up reliably in the literature and that, in everyday situations like the one in the car, produce exactly the pattern the stereotype describes.

The honest summary is that the grandmother is right about averages, wrong to generalize from averages to individuals, and entirely justified in feeling vindicated when, twenty minutes after she suggests consulting the map, her husband finally pulls into a gas station, claims he was about to do so anyway, and asks the cashier for directions. The husband, for his part, is welcome to point out that both sexes are equally capable of thought, that the risk-taking gap is partly cultural, and that overconfidence-without-deliberation has produced most of the world’s major inventions alongside most of its avoidable disasters. He will be correct in every particular, and he will still be lost.
